2019 State Standard of Excellence

Performance Management / Continuous Improvement

Back to
Standard

Did the state or any of its agencies implement a performance management system aligned with their statewide strategic goals; with clear and prioritized outcome-focused goals, program objectives, and measures; and did it consistently collect, analyze, and use data and evidence to improve outcomes, return on investment, and other dimensions of performance?

Why is this important?
Performance management systems are important tools to help state governments monitor and improve customer service, program performance, and outcomes for their residents.

Leading Example

CA

Multiple agencies

Issue 2
Issue 4

California’s Department of Health Care Services is one of several departments that has created a performance dashboard. Other dashboards include the Department of Social Services’s Cal-OAR system, which is a local, data-driven program management system that facilitates continuous improvement of county CalWORKs programs by collecting, analyzing, and disseminating outcomes and best practices. The CalFresh Dashboard, also operated by the Department of Social Services, is a full service, one-stop dashboard used statewide and locally to help inform program delivery. At the enterprise-level, the California Government Operations is linking each of the state’s existing data portals, so that all of the state’s open data sets can be searched on a statewide open data portal.

Promising Examples

CO

Single agency

Issue 2

Since 2012, the Colorado Department of Human Services has implemented a performance management framework, C-Stat to improve delivery of food and cash assistance programs as mandated by a court order. According to the C-Stat website, the framework “moves CDHS to an outcomes-oriented and collaborative approach to affect change at every level. The goals are to collect timely data, increase transparency, conduct regular executive meetings to assess the effectiveness of the strategies, and to identify new performance measures, all in support of continuous quality improvement.” Each division within the department collects and reports performance data. Meetings are held weekly on a rotating basis for each division. The online dashboard contains data on nine systems with reports released quarterly. County level data is included on a dashboard. The Department’s Performance and Peer Exchange program, a peer exchange and learning opportunity, allows counties to learn from each other and produces broader learnings for the field, which are available online. This case study has details on design and implementation for policymakers.

CT

Single agency

Issue 3

EdSight, the Connecticut State Department of Education’s student data portal has the capacity to generate student performance Report Card which provides data on enrollment, absenteeism, suspension/expulsion, expenditures, achievement, accountability, graduation, and college readiness. In addition, Connecticut develops an Early Indication Tool report which provides an individualized student support-level for all students in the state based on longitudinal data and their likelihood of reaching academic milestones. This is a secure report and is available to authorized users in all districts and schools.

KY

Statewide

Issue 1
Issue 2

The Kentucky Center for Statistics (KYSTATS) collects and links data to evaluate the state’s education and workforce efforts. Interactive dashboards are used for continuous improvement and performance management. Examples include the Kentucky Workforce Dashboard, which provides an overview of Kentucky’s workforce and labor market and includes data from 10 partner organizations from the state and local levels; moreover, the quarterly reported data reflect the Workforce Innovation Board’s strategic plan to, “create a workforce development system that is value driven for employers, aligns education with industry demands, prepares Kentuckians for the future of work and drives economic development.”

MN

Statewide

Issue 2

The Minnesota Management and Budget Office convening state agencies and departments for ”state-stat” meetings focused on key population outcomes identified on its public dashboard. In addition to monitoring progress on population outcomes, these meetings are used to monitor program performance and to identify opportunities to enhance collaboration and make new investments. To-date these meetings have been internal to the executive branch, but many of the program measures that are reviewed are published in agency budgets.

UT

Multiple agencies

Issue 2

Utah’s Governor’s Office of Management and Budget (GOMB) developed the SUCCESS framework, which is a performance formula used to calculate work, quality, and expenses across the government functions. In January 2013, the Governor challenged Cabinet-level agencies to hit a 25 percent improvement target over four years. As of the end of 2018, the State exceeded that goal by improving more than 40 percent. GOMB also supports agencies define their performance measures beyond the SUCCESS’s dimensions, with lengths of stay as performance metric fortate hospitals, for example. As an independent agency, the Utah Department of Transportation has brought together data from across the system in a dashboard. Their strategic plan demonstrates the purpose for that dashboard, and, in a State Scoop article, another appeal of the dashboard was explained: “Another part of the appeal, Marshall said, is that the data previously only shared once a year through the agency’s paper-bound ‘strategic direction’ book is now accessible to the public in real time — allowing the department to eliminate the paper version of book entirely.”
